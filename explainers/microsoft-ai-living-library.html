A “Living Library” That Lets You Meet Theodore Roosevelt

The new Theodore Roosevelt Presidential Library uses AI to turn hundreds of thousands of scattered historical documents into a searchable research tool — and a lifelike avatar you can talk to.

SourceMicrosoft Signal

The idea in five lines

  • The Theodore Roosevelt Presidential Library in Medora, North Dakota is designed as a “living library” powered by AI.
  • Roosevelt’s written record sat for a century in 32 separate collections across 18 institutions — readable only by those who could reach the archive.
  • A knowledge base called Box 1 organizes, enriches, and reconstructs fragmented materials into searchable, contextualized records.
  • The Campfire Reading Room lets anyone ask a question in everyday language and get Roosevelt’s actual documents in response.
  • An AI-powered avatar lets visitors chat with a lifelike Roosevelt — with safety protocols keeping it PG-rated.
The concept

From scattered archive to living knowledge base

Microsoft, as lead technology partner through its AI For Good Lab, helped build the knowledge base backbone of the museum. Archivists uploaded hundreds of thousands of archival documents to Box 1, using AI to turn fragmented materials into a coherent, searchable record.

The experiences

Two ways to meet history

Anywhere

The Campfire Reading Room

An AI-powered digital research tool anyone in the world can use to search through Roosevelt’s writings, letters, images, and historical materials — in plain language.

In person

The Roosevelt avatar

Visitors chat with a lifelike AI Roosevelt about his experiences, leadership philosophy, and legacy. When a senator met it, the digital Roosevelt cracked a joke — capturing his real skill for connecting with people.

Safety protocols keep conversations PG-rated for visitors of all ages; the avatar will demur and redirect if asked about something off-limits. Because it’s built on Box 1, the avatar automatically updates as more documents are added or as generative AI improves — which is exactly why the team calls it a “living library.”

Open-sourced for other institutions

Microsoft donated much of its work through the AI For Good Lab, and plans to publish a paper documenting how the technology works and open-source the software — so other cultural institutions can build the same kind of living archive.
